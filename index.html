CASE 01 · EMBEDDED SYSTEMS / ROBOTICS / PCB DESIGN

Landing system for hypothesized surfaces.

An ASU student capstone with NASA Psyche as mission partner: a steel-and-aluminum prototype lander designed to stabilize on unknown terrain and deploy an internal rover through one of six doors.

Design for terrain nobody has seen.

Because 16 Psyche’s surface is not fully characterized, the team had to design around uncertainty: steep slopes, hard impact, and unknown friction. The mechanical concept paired a rounded underbody that could settle toward a stable posture with outward-opening panels that expanded the footprint after landing.

The central electronics problem was just as physical: rotate an internal rover to the correct one of six exits, align it repeatably, and coordinate that motion with the door and indication systems.

One board, two controllers, one integrated system.

I owned the embedded electronics end to end: the custom KiCad PCB, controller integration, motor-drive circuitry, Hall-sensor conditioning, and the firmware required to coordinate the prototype.

  • PIC firmware controlled rover motion and the indexed rotation sequence.
  • PSoC firmware handled door and LED functions, keeping responsibilities explicit across the system.
  • Six fixed magnets created repeatable 60° destinations for Hall-effect position detection.
  • The board integrated the controllers, motor drivers, signal conditioning, power, and system I/O in one testable assembly.
The valuable result was not a perfect first board. It was a documented path from an under-rated driver and Hall sensing that required signal conditioning to a revised PCB that could survive repeated system testing.

Revision was part of the engineering.

Bring-up exposed the real constraints. The team replaced an under-rated motor driver, added Hall-signal conditioning, moved selected op-amps from surface-mount to through-hole parts for practical integration, and revised the PCB to version 1.1. Those changes were driven by bench behavior, not aesthetics.

The result was a system that could be isolated and validated by subsystem—power, controllers, sensing, motor actuation, then coordinated operation—before full mechanical tests.

Measured results, with the boundary stated.

The rover positioning mechanism achieved ±2° accuracy across 500+ automated positioning cycles. The physical lander was tested on slopes through 36°, exceeding the project criterion of greater than 20°.

A SolidWorks 2 m/s impact simulation using a recommended titanium-alloy material assumption reported 1.8 mm maximum deformation and a 2.794 safety factor. Those values do not describe the steel-and-aluminum physical prototype and are distinct from the physical slope and positioning tests.

Follow the evidence.

This was a university proof of concept developed with a mission partner—not NASA flight hardware. The project listing and original showcase material below provide the fuller context.

CASE 02 · EDGE AI / ESP32 / SYSTEM INTEGRATION

Soil-health inference without a cloud dependency.

An EGR 598 SolarSPELL course project that combined live soil sensing, Edge Impulse inference, and an offline ESP-DASH interface on a LILYGO T-Higrow ESP32 platform.

Turn sensor readings into a useful local decision.

SolarSENSE explored a low-cost soil-health tool for small-scale agricultural contexts where connectivity and laboratory access cannot be assumed. The device needed to collect live readings, run inference locally, and present a result from hardware a user could carry into the field.

The model features did not match the board.

The initial data centered on NPK values, but the available T-Higrow hardware did not include NPK sensors. It exposed moisture, electrical-conductivity / salinity, temperature, humidity, and light sensing instead. The team had to pivot the model toward inputs the physical system could actually produce.

That mismatch became the defining engineering decision: prefer a coherent end-to-end system over a stronger-looking model that depended on unavailable measurements.

A model that cannot receive its features from deployed hardware is not an embedded-AI system. The useful work was closing that gap.

Bridge inference, firmware, and interface.

My primary contribution was integrating the Edge Impulse deployment output with the existing ESP32 firmware and ESP-DASH framework. The finished build read live sensors, ran the adapted model on-device, and surfaced both raw values and the resulting soil-health prediction through a browser interface hosted by the board.

  • Mapped the generated model files and dependencies into the Arduino project.
  • Resolved compile and integration issues between the inference code and dashboard firmware.
  • Connected model results to the same local interface that displayed the sensor values.
  • Kept the experience available without a remote cloud service.

A working prototype, not a field trial.

The integrated prototype was demonstrated against prepared soil conditions at the ASU showcase. The supplied reports describe more than one model and dataset iteration, so this portfolio does not collapse them into one headline accuracy figure.

The work was not a deployment in Rwanda. It is presented here as a controlled course prototype rather than generalized field performance.

CASE 03 · MECHATRONICS / OPTICS / HUMAN-CENTERED DESIGN

Four visual modes, one modular platform.

A smart-eyewear proof of concept created with Barrow Neurological Institute / Dignity Health for visual stress and rehabilitation contexts following neurological injury.

Adapt the visual environment to the patient.

People recovering from traumatic brain injury can experience photophobia and visual-processing stress that varies with the environment. Static sunglasses address only one part of that problem. ShadeShift explored a modular platform able to alter tint, hue, stroboscopic training, and binasal occlusion from one wearable form.

Separate the visual modes, unify the platform.

  • Electrochromic lenses provided gradable tint control.
  • Interchangeable color elements supported customizable hues.
  • PDLC film provided the stroboscopic training mode.
  • 3D-printed magnetic attachments created modular binasal occlusion.

An ESP32 controlled the electrochromic tint, while an external enclosure housed the battery, controls, and stroboscopic driver. Iterative 3D printing let the team adjust fit, attachment geometry, and component packaging quickly.

The strongest design decisions came from refusing to hide prototype failures: weight moved the electronics off the frames, and inconsistent PNLC behavior drove a change to PDLC for the stroboscopic mode.

Reduce what the wearer has to carry.

The first packaging direction put too much mass on the eyewear. The controller, battery, and controls moved to an external enclosure, leaving the frames focused on optics and lightweight attachments. The team also transitioned from PNLC to PDLC film when the original material did not produce the required switching behavior reliably.

A prototype for evaluation, not a medical claim.

The project demonstrated integration and design feasibility. It did not establish clinical efficacy, and the research paper explicitly places clinical trials, safety validation, miniaturization, and long-term wearability in future work.

CASE 04 · POWER SYSTEMS / MODEL VALIDATION

Question the optimum before defending the design.

An independent XENDEE course study for a Level I trauma center in Honolulu, focused on the difference between a mathematically optimal result and a physically credible system.

Model a critical load under outage conditions.

The study built a techno-economic microgrid scenario around a major Pacific trauma center. Inputs included the load profile, tariff structure, capital assumptions, renewable generation, battery storage, backup generation, and resilience requirements.

The first answer was too good.

The unconstrained optimization returned roughly 4.98 MW of PV and 12.3 MWh of battery storage. The result minimized the modeled objective, but its footprint raised a practical question against the study’s assumed space limits.

That was the signal to inspect the assumptions rather than celebrate the score. An optimizer will use whatever capacity the model permits if space is never represented as a constraint.

A simulation can be internally correct and still describe a system nobody can build. Physical constraints belong in the model, not in a disclaimer after the result.

Make the scenario answerable in the real world.

The redesigned scenario applied assumed PV and battery space constraints and incorporated diesel generation for extended resilience. That changed the architecture from a PV-plus-storage optimum with no generator into a more physically grounded hybrid scenario.

A disciplined study, not a commissioned design.

This was an academic analysis, not an installed microgrid or a facilities-approved engineering package. Its value is the model-validation process: identify an implausible result, expose the missing constraints, and rebuild the scenario before communicating a recommendation.
