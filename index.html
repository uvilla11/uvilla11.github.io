EGR 401/402 CAPSTONE · TUNGSTEN / IRIDIUM CLASS

Psyche Landing System
for Hypothesized Surfaces

Role Embedded System Design Lead Team Hephaestus · ASU Polytechnic Year 2024

A proof-of-concept asteroid lander for 16-Psyche — a metal world with unknown, possibly steep terrain. I led the electronics: a custom KiCad PCB, every line of C firmware, and a Hall-effect positioning system for the rover inside.

01 Mission 02 The build 03 Validation 04 Stack

The mission

NASA's Psyche mission targets asteroid 16-Psyche, suspected to be the exposed metallic core of a destroyed planetesimal. Because its surface is uncharacterized, the lander must handle steep slopes, hard impacts, and unknown friction — without knowing which it will face first.

Team Hephaestus (four of us across mechanical systems and robotics) chose a steel-and-aluminum lander with a spherical underbelly to roll into a stable posture, plus eight outward-deploying doors that fold down as a wider footprint once landed. The doors release the rover inside.

My piece of it

All electronics, end to end. I drew the board in KiCad — a PIC18 and a PSoC, motor drivers for the door latches, and signal conditioning for the Hall-effect sensors that close the positioning loop on the rover. The mechanical team gave me a steel shell; everything that thinks or moves on command is my firmware.

  • Custom PCB in KiCad carrying PIC and PSoC microcontrollers, motor drivers, and sensor conditioning.
  • Wrote all firmware in embedded C — user input, door latch control, LED indicators, and positioning loops.
  • Hall-effect sensor positioning system that rotates the rover to six named destinations with ±2° accuracy.

Validation

The lander ran 500+ automated cycles of door-deploy / rover-rotate / latch-seal with zero recorded faults. Physically, we tested it on slopes up to 36° — the criterion was greater than 20°, so we came in well above. The Hall-based closed loop held its ±2° the whole time.

EGR 598 · ASU SOLARSPELL INITIATIVE

SolarSENSE
Edge-AI Soil Sensing

Role Team Lead — System Integration Partner SolarSPELL / Rwandan coffee farmers Year 2025

A standalone soil-health monitor that runs a TinyML model on an 8-bit MCU — no cloud, no SIM, no lab. Farmers in Rwanda read live soil conditions off a self-hosted WiFi dashboard from the field.

01 The problem 02 My role 03 The build 04 Results 05 Stack

The problem

Rural coffee farmers in Rwanda have small hillside plots, no lab access, no internet, and nutrient-poor acidic soils. Decisions about irrigation and fertilizer end up being guesses. The team was asked for a tool that costs tens of dollars, asks for no training, and works where there is no router.

My role

I was the integration lead. The simpler part was the build: take a TinyML model trained in Edge Impulse and merge it with the LILYGO T-Higrow's existing ESP-DASH firmware so the board reads soil, runs the model, and serves a WiFi dashboard with the prediction — all from one chip.

The harder part was the pivot. The Rwandan dataset had NPK (nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium). The board only carries moisture and salinity sensors. I led the retrain — throwing out NPK as a feature, switching to salinity+moisture inputs, and rebuilding the model around what the board could actually read in the field.

The build

  • 1,000-row Rwandan dataset cleaned and labeled into four actionable categories: Too Dry, Needs Fertilizer, At Risk, Healthy.
  • Decision-tree model in Edge Impulse, two features (normalized moisture + soil-health score) — small enough for 8-bit flash.
  • Edge Impulse C library merged with the board's ESP-DASH firmware in Arduino IDE. ESP32 then hosts its own WiFi hotspot and dashboard at 192.168.4.1 — phone connects, no internet needed.

Results

Test split accuracy hit 94.4% on the held-out set. In real soil at the Innovation Showcase with four pots of known condition, the board called 9 of 10 readings correctly — misses were soil uniformity and probe placement, not the model.

EGR 555 · COLLABORATION WITH DIGNITY HEALTH

ShadeShift
Therapeutic Eyewear

Role Team Lead Partner Barrow Neurological Institute / Dignity Health Year 2025

A 4-in-1 wearable for patients with photophobia, visual processing disorders, and recovering traumatic brain injuries. Tint, color, stroboscopic training, binasal occlusion — all on one pair of 3D-printed modular frames, driven by an external control module.

01 The clinical need 02 My role 03 The build 04 Design pivots 05 Stack

The clinical need

Patients recovering from a TBI, neurosurgery, or chronic migraine often can't tolerate ordinary lighting. Static tinted sunglasses don't help much: the right lens changes minute by minute. Therapists also use stroboscopic training and binasal occlusion, but those are separate, ugly, clinic-only tools. Our stakeholders wanted one assistive device a patient would actually wear in public.

My role

Team lead — four engineers. I owned the project's direction, the clinical liaison with Dignity Health, and the two critical engineering pivots that kept the project on schedule. I also did the CAD and 3D-printed the modular snap-on frames.

The build

  • 3D-printed modular PLA frames with embedded magnets for clip-on tints, color filters, and binasal occlusion strips.
  • Electrochromic lenses driven by an ESP32 over PWM — user changes tint live with a button.
  • Polymer-dispersed liquid crystal (PDLC) film for fast, reliable stroboscopic visual training.
  • External enclosure carries the ESP32, battery, and controls — tethered to the frames by a thin cable.

Design pivots I led

Two decisions saved the project. First: the onboard electronics made the glasses too heavy — I moved the controller and battery to an external enclosure, trading wire management for wearability. Second: our initial stroboscopic material, PNLC film, needed its controller reverse-engineered and was unreliable across temperature. I led the migration to PDLC film, which gave us fast, consistent switching at the duty cycles the therapy needs.

EGR 586 · INDEPENDENT ANALYSIS

Microgrid Design
for a Critical Facility Load

Role Sole author Site Queen's Medical Center, Honolulu Year 2025

A techno-economic microgrid design for a Level I Trauma Center in Honolulu. Solar PV, battery storage, and diesel generation sized for both economic dispatch and outage resilience — using XENDEE against the local utility's Time-of-Use tariff.

01 The site 02 Approach 03 The suspicious result 04 The defensible redesign 05 Stack

The site

Queen's Medical Center in Honolulu, the Level I Trauma Center for the Pacific basin. Failures during a tropical storm or outage are not an inconvenience — they cost lives. Hawaii's grid is also expensive and time-of-use tariffed, so the economic case for onsite generation is unusually clean.

Approach

Built the model in XENDEE end-to-end alone: load profile for a Level I facility, local Hawaiian Electric tariff, Lazard-reported capital costs for PV / BESS / diesel, and Time-of-Use dispatch logic. Ran four scenarios — an economic Best Case, a Low Asset Cost case, a 100% Renewable mandate, and 1-day / 3-day / 2-week outage resilience scenarios.

The suspicious result

The Best Case was too good. It sailed through the 2-week outage with no added cost, no generator at all — just oversized PV and BESS. That does not match how real hospital roofs constrain physical space. I didn't accept the result.

The defensible redesign

I imposed realistic rooftop-area constraints on the PV and BESS, re-ran the optimizer, and got the answer a hospital facilities team would actually sign off on: a smaller renewable build that handles economic dispatch, with diesel generation sized for the multi-day outage cases the renewables can't cover alone. The defensible design is the one I'd ship to the client.

EGR 314 · TEAM 305

Home Room
Weather Control System

Role Embedded System Design Lead Team 4 — Diego, Uriah, Jack, Nicholas Year 2023

Open and close the blinds for you. Reads light and temperature, drives a motor, talks over Wi-Fi — all on a custom board the team laid out in Cadence.

01 The idea 02 The build 03 Cross-team work 04 Stack

The idea

If it's too bright outside, close the blinds. If it's hot inside but cool outside, open them. The project brief required multiple sensors and two-way Wi-Fi communication; we picked a use case that turns that spec into something a real household would own — a small appliance that just keeps the room comfortable without a thermostat war.

The build

  • Custom PCB laid out in Cadence: ESP32 module, I2C sensor inputs, motor driver, voltage regulation.
  • Embedded C firmware on a PIC18 via MPLAB X for sensor acquisition, I2C, motor control, and register-level performance optimization.
  • ESP32 programmed in Python for MQTT — remote monitoring and control over Wi-Fi, so a phone can open and close the blinds.
  • Lessons from this build fed directly into my NASA Psyche PCB work a semester later: pad sizing, header placement, and never trusting a single sensor reading.

Cross-team work

Four-person team. I owned hardware development and led technical decision-making for the board, with three teammates focused on subsystem design, software, system verification, and the tabletop demo frame. Final result was a working prototype plus a fully documented project site.
