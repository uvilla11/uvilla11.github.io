EGR 401/402 CAPSTONE · TUNGSTEN / IRIDIUM CLASS

Psyche Landing System
for Hypothesized Surfaces

Role Embedded System Design LeadTeam Hephaestus · ASU PolytechnicYear 2024

A proof-of-concept asteroid lander for 16-Psyche — a metal world with unknown, possibly steep terrain. I led the electronics: a custom KiCad PCB, every line of C firmware, and a Hall-effect positioning system for the rover inside.

01 Mission02 The build03 Validation04 Stack05 Resources

The mission

NASA's Psyche mission targets asteroid 16-Psyche, suspected to be the exposed metallic core of a destroyed planetesimal. Because its surface is uncharacterized, the lander must handle steep slopes, hard impacts, and unknown friction — without knowing which it will face first.

Team Hephaestus chose a steel-and-aluminum lander with a spherical underbelly — literally a salad bowl cut and repurposed as the base — to roll into a stable posture, plus eight outward-deploying doors that fold down as a wider footprint once landed.

My piece of it

All electronics, end to end. I drew the board in KiCad — a PIC18 and a PSoC, motor drivers for the door latches, and signal conditioning for the Hall-effect sensors that close the positioning loop on the rover.

  • Custom PCB in KiCad carrying PIC and PSoC microcontrollers, motor drivers, and sensor conditioning.
  • Wrote all firmware in embedded C — user input, door latch control, LED indicators, and positioning loops.
  • Hall-effect sensor positioning system that rotates the rover to six named destinations with ±2° accuracy.

Validation

The lander ran 500+ automated cycles of door-deploy / rover-rotate / latch-seal with zero recorded faults. Physically, we tested it on slopes up to 36° — the criterion was greater than 20°.

BLOG · MAR 2025

How I Got ±2° Rover Positioning
with Hall Sensors

Read time 8 minTags Hall-effect, Firmware, PIC18

The Psyche lander needed to point its rover at one of six doors, rotate to it, and send it out — all with ±2° accuracy. The solution was a Hall-effect sensor indexed by six fixed magnets.

The physics

A Hall-effect sensor produces a voltage proportional to the magnetic field passing through it. By placing six magnets at known angular positions, the sensor detects which magnet it's nearest — and by interpolating between two, determines exact angular position.

The firmware

The PIC18 reads the Hall sensor via ADC at a fixed sample rate. A lookup table maps voltage readings to angular positions. Closed-loop control: read position, compute error, drive motor, repeat. PID loop at 100Hz.

Calibration

The real work was calibration. Every magnet is slightly different. Every mounting position has tolerance. I wrote a calibration routine that rotates through 360°, records Hall voltage at each position, and builds a per-unit lookup table. This made ±2° repeatable across 500+ cycles.

TinyML on an 8-Bit MCU:
What Actually Fits in Flash

Read time 10 minTags TinyML, Edge Impulse, ESP32

Everyone talks about TinyML on edge devices. Fewer talk about what happens when your "edge device" has 28KB of flash and 2KB of RAM.

The constraint

The LILYGO T-Higrow uses an ESP32, but the project brief required running inference on the smallest possible footprint. Edge Impulse generates C libraries for 8-bit architectures, but the model has to fit.

Feature selection

The Rwandan dataset had NPK values. The board only reads moisture and salinity. I threw out three features and rebuilt the model around what the hardware could actually measure.

The model

A decision tree with two normalized inputs. No neural network — decision trees are fast, deterministic, and tiny. Edge Impulse generated the C inference code, merged with ESP-DASH firmware.

BLOG · JAN 2025

Building a PCB in KiCad:
From Schematic to Manufacture

Read time 12 minTags KiCad, PCB Design, Hardware

The Psyche capstone PCB was the first board I designed from scratch that had to actually work.

Component selection

PIC18 for main controller. PSoC for motor driver interface. Dual motor drivers. Hall-effect sensor conditioning. Voltage regulation from 12V to 3.3V and 5V rails.

Layout decisions

Biggest lesson: pad sizing matters. Undersized motor driver pads in first revision. Second lesson: header placement — put them where scope probes can actually reach during bring-up.

Lessons

Never trust a single sensor reading. Add decoupling capacitors everywhere. Route power traces wide. Always do a design review with someone who has built boards before.

BLOG · DEC 2024

Why I Questioned My Own
XENDEE Results

Read time 6 minTags XENDEE, Power Systems

When your microgrid model says "no generator needed for a 2-week outage," the right response is suspicion, not celebration.

The too-good result

XENDEE's optimizer found oversized PV and battery that handled every scenario without diesel. On paper, perfect. In reality, more panels than a hospital rooftop could fit.

Adding constraints

Imposed realistic rooftop-area constraints on PV and BESS. Result: smaller renewable build for economic dispatch, diesel sized for multi-day outages.

The lesson

Models are only as good as their constraints. If the answer seems too good, the constraints are probably wrong. Always sanity-check against physical reality.
