# **Project 1: EGR 598 SolarSENSE Soil Monitoring for Rwandan Coffee Farmers**

The goal of this project was to address the significant challenges faced by rural coffee farmers in Rwanda, who often lack access to affordable, simple, and practical tools for soil health monitoring (Pisors et al.). The primary objective was to develop a low-cost, stand-alone system that provides farmers with real-time, actionable insights to improve crop yields and optimize resource management in environments with limited connectivity (Pisors et al.). As a team, we developed an IoT-based sensor solution using the LILYGO T-Higrow ESP32 board. The system was designed to leverage Tiny Machine Learning (TinyML) to provide on-device soil quality predictions without requiring an internet connection. The overall process involved analyzing a soil dataset provided by our Rwandan partners, training a classification model using the Edge Impulse platform, and afterward deploying that lightweight model directly onto the ESP32 microcontroller ([see Appendix A](#appendix-a:-egr-598-team-final-report)).

My central role in this team project was the significant technical task of system integration. I was specifically responsible for merging the exported TinyML model code from Edge Impulse with the LILYGO T-Higrow's existing firmware. This firmware was already configured to use the ESP DASH framework to host a web-based dashboard on the ESP32 itself. My work began with collaborating on the initial creation and labeling of data for our ML model. A critical challenge emerged when we realized the initial NPK (Nitrogen, Phosphorus, Potassium) dataset did not match our board's available sensors. I then focused on pivoting our model's design, leading the effort to retrain and validate a new model that successfully used the board's onboard salinity (EC) and moisture sensors, ultimately achieving over 94% accuracy. Following this, I led the complex integration of the new model's code with the ESP DASH firmware inside the Arduino IDE. This iterative process was a demanding process where I utilized Google Gemini to understand the complex file structure and to effectively debug compilation and runtime errors, which significantly accelerated development (Villa, *Final Individual Reflection on TinyML and Soil Sensing in the SolarSENSE Project*).

The outcome was a functional, integrated system. I successfully flashed the combined code onto the LILYGO T-Higrow board, resulting in a device that could read live soil data (salinity and moisture), process it locally through the integrated TinyML model, and display the final soil health prediction (e.g., "Too Dry," "Healthy") alongside the raw sensor data on its self-hosted ESP DASH web dashboard. The SolarSENSE product provided a complete, offline "sense-and-predict" solution for the farmers ([see Appendix B](#appendix-b:-egr-598-final-individual-reflection)).

# **Project 2: EGR 555 ShadeShift: Smart Modular Eyewear for Neurological Recovery**

This project, developed in collaboration with Dignity Health, addressed the clinical need for a holistic solution for patients recovering from traumatic brain injuries (TBIs) who suffer from photophobia and visual processing disorders. The primary objective was to design and prototype "ShadeShift," a 4-in-1 therapeutic device featuring customizable color hues, gradable electrochromic tints, stroboscopic training, and binasal occlusion technology, all integrated into a wearable, all-in-one modular eyewear system (Villa et al.).

As the team lead, I was responsible for guiding the project's direction and executing the core design and integration. The team's process involved iterative CAD design, 3D printing of frames, and integrating electro-optic materials with a microcontroller. I learned and applied 3D modeling and printing to develop the custom, lightweight snap-on frames that enabled the modularity for binasal occlusion and customizable color therapies.

A significant part of my contribution involved resolving some critical engineering challenges in our prototype. Initially, the weight of the onboard electronics made the glasses uncomfortable and impractical. I solved this by redesigning the hardware architecture, moving the microcontroller and battery to a compact external enclosure. Furthermore, our initial material for stroboscopic training, PNLC (Polymer Network Liquid Crystal) film, proved unreliable as the controller for the film needed to be reverse-engineered entirely for our use case. I led the transition to Polymer Dispersed Liquid Crystal (PDLC) film, which was essential to achieving the reliable and fast-switching stroboscopic effects required for visual therapy.

The outcome was a successful, fully functional prototype and proof of concept for our idea. The ShadeShift system successfully demonstrated all four therapeutic functions in a single, integrated device. The final assembly successfully demonstrated a proof-of-concept for the electrochromic lenses, which control tint, and the 3D-printed magnetic clip-ons for the colored filters, binasal occlusion, and the PDLC film for stroboscopic training, thereby validating the modular and multi-functional design (Uriah Villa, 2025\) ([see Appendix C](#appendix-c:-egr-555-shadeshift-team-final-report)).

## **Reflection on Project 2**

This project represented a significant accomplishment in systems integration and engineering problem-solving. My role as team lead required me not only to manage the project's goals but also to delve into materials science and hardware architecture to identify and solve fundamental design flaws. The decision to pivot from an all-in-one frame to an external enclosure, along with the research and testing that led to the transition from PNLC to PDLC film, were critical leadership and engineering decisions that saved the project and ensured its completion and success within the allotted class and project duration.

On a technical level, I gained new expertise in additive manufacturing (3D printing) and rapid prototyping, moving from a CAD concept in Tinkercad to a functional, user-tested device. This project was a definitive example of mechatronics, forcing me to integrate electronics (microcontrollers, PWM signals, and analog signals), optics (PDLC and electrochromic films), and mechanical design (3D-printed modular frames).

The most significant aspect of this project was its interdisciplinary nature. I served as the team's primary point of contact with Dignity Health, enabling me to collaborate directly with clinical stakeholders. This experience taught me how to translate clinical needs into concrete engineering specifications, ultimately leading to a holistic solution. It solidified my ability to bridge the gap between medical engineering, patient-centric design, and mechatronic engineering.

# **Project 3: EGR 586 Microgrid Design for a Critical Facility Load**

This project was an independent analysis to design and optimize a microgrid for a critical facility, The Queen's Medical Center in Honolulu, Hawaii. The primary objective was to use an industry-standard software platform, XENDEE, to model a microgrid that could provide both economic savings against the utility's Time-of-Use (TOU) tariff and enhance energy resilience during grid outages of varying durations (Villa, *EGR 586 Project 1*). My process involved the complete, independent execution of the microgrid design. I began by identifying the critical facility, researching its function as a Level I Trauma Center, and selecting an appropriate load profile for the facility. I then built the project in XENDEE, which involved defining all financial and technical parameters, including the complex local utility tariff, demand charges, and the capital costs for Solar PV, Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS), and diesel generators.

I independently ran and analyzed multiple scenarios: a "Best Case" for economic optimization, a "Low Asset Cost" case where each asset was the lowest possible cost according to the Lazard Report, a "100% Renewable" mandate, and a series of resilience scenarios for 1-day, 3-day, and 2-week outages. My initial analysis revealed that the "Best Case" economic design was surprisingly robust, capable of handling a 2-week outage with no added cost. Skeptical of this "perfect" result, I conducted a second analysis with more realistic constraints on solar and battery physical space, which produced a more nuanced and practical design that incorporated the diesel generator for extended outages ([see Appendix D](#appendix-d:-egr-586-project-1-report)).

## **Reflection on Project 3**

This project was a significant demonstration of my independent analytical abilities. It directly reflects my growth in engineering knowledge by requiring me to understand and model the complex interplay between economic optimization (NPV, LCOE) and engineering resilience for a critical societal asset. I learned the fundamentals of microgrid design, load-profile analysis, and the dispatch logic of hybrid systems (PV \+ BESS and Generator).

This second, self-motivated investigation demonstrated a deeper, more practical understanding of the engineering problem and led to a more realistic and defensible microgrid design.
